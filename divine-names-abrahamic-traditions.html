Divine Names and Attributes

A Comparative Abrahamic Study

The study of divine names and attributes stands among the most central theological subjects within the Abrahamic traditions. Across the Torah, the Gospel traditions, and the Qur’an, divine names function not merely as religious titles, but as theological structures expressing the nature of God, revelation, authority, sacred presence, covenant, judgment, mercy, power, and the relationship between the divine and humanity.

This project approaches divine names through a comparative textual, linguistic, theological, and historical framework. The study examines how the Abrahamic scriptures construct the image of God through sacred naming traditions, divine attributes, revelation language, and theological description.

Particular attention will be given to:

  • the divine names in the Torah,
  • the sacred name YHWH and its theological significance,
  • Elohim, El Shaddai, and covenantal naming structures,
  • divine names and attributes in the Gospel traditions,
  • the language of Fatherhood and sacred mediation,
  • the Qur’anic divine name “Allah”,
  • Asma’ Allah al-Husna (The Beautiful Names of God),
  • divine mercy, judgment, transcendence, and sacred authority,
  • the relationship between divine names and revelation,
  • semantic development of divine terminology,
  • divine attributes and theological interpretation,
  • the relationship between sacred language and divine presence,
  • and the continuity and transformation of divine naming across the Abrahamic traditions.

The project will also examine the theological tensions surrounding divine attributes, particularly concerning transcendence and anthropomorphic language, sacred mediation, divine speech, divine visibility and invisibility, and the development of theological interpretation across religious history.

Through comparative analysis, this study seeks to understand how divine names became foundational structures for theology, worship, law, spirituality, prophetic authority, and religious identity throughout the Abrahamic world.
