The Definition of God

A Comparative Abrahamic Study

1 — The Definition of God in the Torah

This study approaches the Torah as the foundational source in the formation of the Abrahamic conception of God within the religious and historical structure of the Hebrew tradition.

Accordingly, the research is based on the principle of scholarly integrity in presenting the texts as they appear, without selective omission, reconstruction, or the imposition of later theological assumptions upon the original material.

The purpose of this section is not to construct a new image of God, nor to issue doctrinal judgments, but rather to trace the image presented by the Torah itself and to examine its internal structure, including both its stable theological foundations and the tensions that emerge within the textual portrayal.

This section begins by establishing the Ten Commandments as one of the central foundational texts for understanding the relationship between God and humanity within the Torah.

The commandments are not presented merely as legal instructions, but as a declaration of divine identity itself. The text begins with God identifying Himself as the one who brought the Israelites out of bondage, thereby connecting worship, covenant, obedience, morality, and law to the divine nature. In this sense, the Ten Commandments function as a primary theological framework for understanding the conception of God within the Torah.

Through a comprehensive reading of the Torah from beginning to end, God is presented as the one and unique deity without rival or partner, the creator of the heavens, the earth, humanity, and life itself.

The Torah portrays God as the origin of existence and the supreme authority over creation, nature, and history. Creation is linked directly to divine action, and the power of God is manifested through the formation, ordering, sustaining, and governing of the world.

The Torah further presents God as eternal, associated with the patriarchs and covenants, and continuous throughout generations. God appears not as a distant abstraction separated from history, but as an active and intervening presence within human events. The divine role is expressed through revelation, prophecy, legislation, judgment, salvation, covenant, and historical intervention.

Within the Torah, God is described as:

  • the One God,
  • the Creator,
  • the Holy One,
  • the Just,
  • the Merciful,
  • the Forgiving,
  • the Jealous God,
  • the Lord of the Covenant,
  • the Lawgiver,
  • the Judge,
  • the Guide,
  • the Hearer of prayer,
  • and the Deliverer of His people.

The Torah also presents God as the source of law and legislation. Divine commandments regulate not only ritual worship, but also moral and social life, including prohibitions against murder, injustice, adultery, falsehood, and oppression. As a result, the image of God within the Torah becomes deeply connected to ethical order and social justice alongside holiness and worship.

The texts further emphasize divine knowledge of human actions and intentions, as well as God’s continuous involvement in human affairs through revelation, prophetic speech, commandments, punishments, deliverance, and historical events. God is portrayed as the speaking deity who reveals His will to prophets, places words into the mouths of messengers, and guides the religious community through direct revelation or mediated communication.

The image of God in the Torah is also deeply connected to the concept of holiness. Divine presence appears in specific sacred settings such as Mount Sinai, the Tabernacle, the pillar of cloud and fire, and the Ark of the Covenant. Holiness is associated with fear, reverence, ritual purity, and strict boundaries governing worship and approach to the sacred. Approaching the holy sometimes appears as a dangerous act requiring precise conditions and limitations. This theme will be examined in greater detail in a later independent section due to the central role holiness plays in shaping the relationship between God and humanity within the Torah.

Within the framework of the divine-human relationship, the Torah presents God as the Lord of covenantal relationships, particularly with Noah, Abraham, and the Israelites. The covenant functions as a religious, moral, and legislative bond grounded in worship, obedience, commitment, promise, blessing, protection, and punishment simultaneously.

God also appears as the Deliverer and Savior, especially in the Exodus narrative, where divine identity is tied not merely to abstract theology but to historical action, liberation, and salvation. In addition, God is portrayed as the ruler of history and the director of nations and peoples, raising kingdoms and bringing them down according to the unfolding religious narrative.

Alongside these central and relatively stable descriptions, however, the Torah also contains passages that raise interpretive and theological questions concerning the nature of divine description itself. Certain texts employ anthropomorphic or sensory language in relation to God, including descriptions of walking, descending, hands, face, eyes, anger, direct speech, and localized appearances. Such descriptions open important discussions regarding the nature of religious language within the Torah and whether these expressions are intended literally or function as accommodative language intended to communicate divine realities through human perception.

Other passages speak of divine “regret” or describe changes in the course of punishment following prayer or repentance, raising questions concerning divine will, immutability, and narrative representation within the Torah. Tension also appears between texts emphasizing divine transcendence and incomparability and others that describe visible manifestations or experiences associated with the divine presence.

The Torah further contains narratives involving direct dialogue between God and human beings, such as Abraham’s intercession concerning Sodom, Moses’ objections and appeals, and Jacob’s wrestling encounter. These narratives raise broader questions regarding the nature of divine-human interaction and the limits of narrative representation of the divine presence.

An especially significant issue concerns the figure of the “Angel of the Lord,” where some passages present a fluid movement between the speech of the angel and the speech of God Himself. At times the narrative shifts between “the angel” and “the Lord” without a clear distinction, opening important avenues for studying mediation, revelation, and divine representation within ancient religious texts. This may indicate that certain forms of divine speech are presented through a mediating figure who speaks fully in the name of God.

The Torah also includes passages concerning collective punishment, religious warfare, destruction, curses, and divine testing of human beings. These texts require careful historical, linguistic, and theological examination in order to understand their relationship to the broader image of God presented within the Torahic narrative structure.

These passages are not presented here as material for rejecting the text, but rather as integral components of the overall structure through which the Torah constructs its conception of God. Accordingly, the study does not engage in selective citation, but seeks to gather the full textual image as presented, including its central attributes, religious functions, linguistic expressions, and theological tensions.

For this reason, the study of the definition of God in the Torah extends beyond merely collecting divine names and attributes. It also includes analyzing the relationship between divine transcendence and historical presence, holiness and mercy, justice and punishment, revelation and mediation, as well as stability and change within religious expression.

This section will later be accompanied by detailed tables concerning the names and attributes of God as they appear throughout the Torah, including their meanings, contexts, and theological functions within the narrative structure, before moving in later stages of the project toward the study of the definition of God in the Gospel and finally the Qur’an as part of a broader investigation into the conception of God across the Abrahamic traditions.

The Gospel Tradition

The Definition of God in the Gospel

The Gospel traditions continue the Abrahamic understanding of God while presenting theological themes connected to mercy, guidance, revelation, spiritual relationship, judgment, and divine presence. The foundation of divine unity appears clearly in the words attributed to Jesus:

“Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is One.”

This declaration preserves continuity with the earlier biblical understanding of God as the one supreme divine reality and the source of revelation, authority, creation, and sacred law.

Within the Gospel narrative, God is frequently described through the language of “Fatherhood.” However, the concept of divine fatherhood does not necessarily imply biological generation. Rather, it often functions within a theological and spiritual framework describing God as:

  • the sustainer,
  • the guide,
  • the caretaker,
  • the educator,
  • and the source of mercy and moral authority over creation.

The paternal language reflects relationship, guidance, care, discipline, and spiritual belonging rather than physical descent. This symbolic and theological usage appears throughout the broader biblical tradition where divine fatherhood may refer to covenantal closeness, protection, authority, or spiritual instruction.

The Gospel portrayal of God includes several major themes that align with earlier Abrahamic conceptions of divine reality:

  • God as One.
  • God as Creator.
  • God as the source of revelation.
  • God as merciful and forgiving.
  • God as judge over humanity.
  • God as spiritually present yet unseen.
  • God as the sustainer of life.
  • God as the source of sacred law and moral authority.
  • God as the hearer of prayer.
  • God as transcendent yet active within history.
  • God as sender of prophets and messengers.
  • God as possessing supreme authority over creation and the kingdom.

The Gospel traditions also emphasize themes connected to:

  • love,
  • mercy,
  • forgiveness,
  • repentance,
  • salvation,
  • and spiritual purification.

At the same time, the Gospel material contains theological expressions and narrative tensions that later became subjects of major doctrinal interpretation and debate concerning the nature of God, divine mediation, sonship language, incarnation, authority, and the relationship between God and Jesus.

These subjects will be examined separately in later sections due to their theological complexity and their central importance within the historical development of Christian doctrine.

Additional themes that will be explored in later sections include:

  • divine sonship language,
  • the relationship between God and Christ,
  • the role of the Holy Spirit,
  • the Paraclete traditions,
  • revelation and divine speech,
  • mediation and authority,
  • worship and prayer,
  • miraculous power,
  • kingdom theology,
  • and the continuity of revelation across the Abrahamic traditions.
The Qur’anic Tradition

The Definition of God in the Qur’an

The divine discourse in the Qur’an begins with the affirmation of absolute monotheism, transcendence, sovereignty, mercy, revelation, and direct authority over creation and history. The Qur’an presents God as the one eternal divine reality without partner, rival, origin, or offspring.

The divine name “Allah” appears as the central and supreme name within the Qur’anic revelation, referring to the one God who alone possesses creation, authority, worship, revelation, and judgment. The name is not presented merely as a religious title, but as the most comprehensive expression of ultimate divine reality within the Qur’anic worldview.

The Qur’anic understanding of God is built upon the concept of Tawhid, where God is described as:

  • the Creator,
  • the Sustainer,
  • the King,
  • the Judge,
  • the Guide,
  • the Most Merciful,
  • and the source of revelation and ultimate truth.

The Qur’an repeatedly emphasizes that God:

  • does not resemble creation,
  • was not born,
  • does not beget,
  • and possesses complete authority over existence, life, death, judgment, and destiny.

Within the Qur’anic framework, divine transcendence is combined with direct involvement in history, revelation, guidance, and human accountability. God in the Qur’an is described as:

  • hearing,
  • seeing,
  • knowing,
  • speaking,
  • guiding,
  • forgiving,
  • punishing,
  • sustaining,
  • and responding to prayer.

The Qur’an also places major emphasis upon the divine names and attributes, where these names function not merely as titles, but as theological expressions revealing the relationship between God, creation, revelation, mercy, justice, wisdom, and power.

Among the major divine names and attributes appearing in the Qur’an and prophetic tradition are:

  • Al-Rahman (The Most Merciful),
  • Al-Rahim (The Compassionate),
  • Al-Malik (The King),
  • Al-Quddus (The Holy),
  • Al-Salam (The Source of Peace),
  • Al-Aziz (The Almighty),
  • Al-Khaliq (The Creator),
  • Al-Alim (The All-Knowing),
  • Al-Sami' (The All-Hearing),
  • Al-Basir (The All-Seeing),
  • Al-Ghafur (The Forgiving),
  • Al-Hakim (The Wise),
  • Al-Haqq (The Truth),
  • Al-Hafiz (The Protector),
  • Al-Hayy (The Living),
  • Al-Qayyum (The Self-Sustaining),
  • Al-Awwal (The First),
  • Al-Akhir (The Last),
  • Al-Zahir (The Manifest),
  • and Al-Batin (The Hidden).

The Qur’anic revelation also strongly rejects:

  • partnership with God,
  • incarnation,
  • biological divine sonship,
  • and the deification of prophets or human beings,

while maintaining the concepts of revelation, prophecy, and prophetic mediation within the limits of servitude and obedience to God alone.

Additional subjects will be explored in later sections of the project, including:

  • revelation and divine speech,
  • the role of spirit and angels,
  • the relationship between God and the prophets,
  • the throne and divine presence,
  • mercy and punishment,
  • destiny and divine decree,
  • divine names and attributes,
  • Abrahamic continuity,
  • and the structure of revelation in comparison with the Torah and the Gospel traditions.

Section I

Introduction to the Definition of God in the Abrahamic Traditions

The question of the definition of God stands as one of the most central and complex subjects within the history of the Abrahamic traditions. The concept of God does not appear within the sacred texts merely as an abstract theological idea, but rather as the foundational reality around which revelation, prophecy, law, sacred history, religious authority, morality, and human identity are constructed. This project approaches the study of God through the direct examination of the Abrahamic scriptures themselves, focusing upon the textual, linguistic, theological, and historical structures through which the image of God is formed across the Torah, the Gospel traditions, and the Qur’an. The purpose of this research is not to promote a sectarian position nor to attack a particular religious tradition. Instead, the study seeks to analyze how God is presented within the texts themselves and how the theological image of God developed throughout Abrahamic religious history. The research is based upon the principle of collecting the complete textual image without selective omission, reconstruction, or doctrinal filtering. Accordingly, the study examines:

  • divine names,
  • attributes,
  • divine actions,
  • revelation and sacred speech,
  • divine presence,
  • prophetic mediation,
  • religious authority,
  • and the language used in describing God.

The project also investigates major theological tensions appearing within the texts themselves, particularly concerning:

  • transcendence and incarnation,
  • presence and absence,
  • direct speech and mediation,
  • mercy and punishment,
  • immutability and change,
  • and the relationship between God and human history.

The study treats the Abrahamic scriptures as interconnected religious and historical structures that developed across multiple stages of revelation, interpretation, transmission, and theological reconstruction. As a result, the study of God becomes connected not only to theology, but also to language, history, sacred authority, and the development of religious thought. For this reason, the project extends beyond the simple question:

“Who is God?”

The study also explores broader questions such as:

  • How do the Abrahamic texts define God?
  • How does God appear within the structure of revelation?
  • How did the image of God develop historically?
  • What is the relationship between God, revelation, and sacred speech?
  • How is religious authority connected to the concept of God?
  • And does a continuous Abrahamic theological structure exist across the Torah, the Gospel, and the Qur’an?

The research will gradually move from the general presentation of God toward detailed textual analysis, beginning with the Torah, followed by the Gospel traditions, and finally the Qur’an, while constructing comparative analytical frameworks intended to examine both continuity and theological transformation across the Abrahamic traditions.

Section II

The Definition of God in the Torah

The Torah stands as the foundational textual source in the formation of the Abrahamic conception of God within the religious and historical structure of ancient Israelite tradition. Within the Torah, God is presented not merely as an abstract philosophical principle, but as the living divine reality who creates, commands, reveals, judges, guides, establishes covenants, intervenes in history, and governs both humanity and creation itself. The Torah introduces God as the one supreme deity without rival or partner, the creator of the heavens, the earth, humanity, life, and sacred order. Divine authority extends over nature, nations, kings, history, morality, and revelation. God appears throughout the Torah as both transcendent above creation and actively present within historical events. The divine identity within the Torah is deeply connected to covenantal relationships, especially through the narratives of Noah, Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, and the Israelites. Through these covenant structures, God is portrayed as:

  • the Creator,
  • the One God,
  • the Lord of the Covenant,
  • the Lawgiver,
  • the Deliverer,
  • the Judge,
  • the Holy One,
  • the Merciful,
  • the Guide,
  • and the ruler of history.

The Torah further presents God as directly involved in revelation and sacred speech. God speaks to prophets, establishes commandments, reveals laws, sends warnings, issues judgments, and intervenes through historical events. Revelation is portrayed both as direct divine communication and as mediated speech delivered through prophetic or angelic figures. One of the central foundations for understanding God within the Torah appears in the Ten Commandments, where divine identity is connected directly to liberation, covenant, law, worship, and moral authority. In this framework, God is not separated from history, but is revealed through action, commandment, and covenantal relationship. The Torah also emphasizes holiness as one of the central dimensions of divine reality. Sacred presence appears through locations and symbols such as:

  • Mount Sinai,
  • the Tabernacle,
  • the Ark of the Covenant,
  • the pillar of cloud and fire,
  • and the divine glory.

Holiness within the Torah is associated with reverence, purity, fear, ritual boundaries, sacred law, and restricted access to the divine presence. At the same time, the Torah contains theological and linguistic complexities concerning the nature of divine description itself. Certain passages employ anthropomorphic language regarding God, including references to divine speech, anger, descent, face, hands, walking, and localized appearances. These passages raise major interpretive questions regarding the relationship between divine transcendence and narrative representation. Additional tensions also emerge concerning:

  • divine presence and invisibility,
  • direct revelation and mediation,
  • justice and mercy,
  • immutability and divine “regret,”
  • and the relationship between God and the Angel of the Lord.

Particular importance will be given to passages involving mediation between God and humanity, especially texts where the speech of the Angel of the Lord appears intertwined with the speech of God Himself. These passages form one of the major foundations for later theological developments concerning revelation, mediation, divine representation, and sacred authority. The Torah also constructs a comprehensive religious worldview in which God functions not only as creator and ruler, but also as the source of:

  • law,

Section III

The Theology of Divine Presence and Holiness in the Torah

The Theology of Divine Presence and Holiness in the Torah

The Torah emphasizes holiness as one of the central dimensions of divine reality. Divine presence appears through sacred locations, covenantal structures, ritual boundaries, and manifestations associated with revelation and worship. Within the Torahic framework, holiness is connected to separation, reverence, purity, sacred law, and regulated access to the divine presence. Sacred presence appears in locations and symbols such as: - Mount Sinai, - the Tabernacle, - the Ark of the Covenant, - the pillar of cloud and fire, - and the divine glory. Holiness within the Torah is associated with: - fear, - reverence, - ritual purity, - sacred boundaries, - covenantal obedience, - priestly mediation, - and restricted approach to the holy. The Torah portrays divine holiness not merely as an abstract attribute, but as an active force shaping religious law, sacred space, worship, priesthood, purification, and communal identity. Access to the divine presence often appears regulated through commandments, ritual conditions, and covenantal structures. This theological framework also introduces major interpretive questions concerning the relationship between transcendence and divine manifestation, sacred presence and invisibility, mediation and direct revelation, and the limits of human approach to the holy. Later stages of this research will examine these themes in greater textual and theological depth, particularly the tensions connected to holiness, sacred space, divine manifestation, mediation, ritual restriction, and the structure of revelation within the Torahic tradition.

Section IV

The Structure of Divine Revelation in the Torah

Within the Torah, revelation appears as one of the primary foundations through which divine authority, sacred law, covenantal structure, and religious identity are established. Revelation is not presented merely as abstract inspiration, but as direct divine communication entering human history through speech, commandment, vision, mediation, covenant, and sacred encounter. The Torah portrays God as a speaking deity who communicates with prophets, patriarchs, priests, leaders, and the covenant community. Divine speech appears through multiple forms, including: - direct speech, - prophetic revelation, - dreams, - visions, - sacred signs, - angelic mediation, - covenantal commandments, - and historical intervention. One of the central characteristics of revelation within the Torah is the connection between divine speech and covenantal authority. Revelation establishes law, regulates worship, defines morality, organizes communal identity, and shapes the structure of sacred history. The Torah also presents revelation as connected to sacred space and sacred presence. Major revelatory events occur through locations such as: - Mount Sinai, - the Tabernacle, - the Tent of Meeting, - and the Ark of the Covenant. Revelation within the Torah is frequently associated with holiness, fear, ritual preparation, and restricted access to the divine presence. Certain forms of revelation appear direct, while others occur through mediating figures such as prophets or the Angel of the Lord. Particular importance is given to passages where divine speech and mediated speech appear intertwined, especially in narratives involving angelic figures who speak fully in the name of God. These passages raise major theological and linguistic questions concerning mediation, representation, authority, and the structure of revelation itself. The Torah also presents revelation as progressive across historical stages. Revelation unfolds gradually through covenants, prophetic encounters, commandments, historical crises, judgment, deliverance, and the formation of sacred law. Later sections of this research will examine: - direct and mediated revelation, - prophetic speech, - sacred law and revelation, - angelic mediation, - revelation and covenant, - revelation and divine presence, - revelation and sacred language, - and the continuity of revelation across the Abrahamic traditions.

Section V

The Angel of the Lord and Divine Mediation in the Torah

One of the most significant theological and textual subjects within the Torah concerns the figure commonly identified as “the Angel of the Lord.” Certain passages present this figure not merely as a separate messenger, but as a mediating presence through whom divine speech, authority, revelation, and sacred action are communicated. In multiple narratives, the text moves fluidly between the speech of the Angel of the Lord and the speech of God Himself. At times, the narrative begins with the angel speaking, yet the language transitions directly into divine first-person speech without clear distinction between messenger and sender. This phenomenon appears in several major Torahic narratives, including: - the encounter with Hagar, - the binding of Isaac, - the burning bush narrative, - wilderness revelation traditions, - and covenantal encounters involving patriarchs and prophets. These passages raise important questions regarding: - divine mediation, - sacred representation, - revelation structure, - prophetic communication, - angelic authority, - and the relationship between transcendence and historical manifestation. Within the Torahic structure, the Angel of the Lord often functions as a visible or mediated form of divine interaction within human history while preserving the transcendence of God beyond direct human perception. The subject also connects closely to: - revelation and sacred speech, - divine authority, - covenant mediation, - prophetic experience, - sacred presence, - and the development of later theological traditions concerning mediation and revelation. Particular attention will later be given to passages where: - the angel speaks as God, - divine names are associated with the angel, - worship or fear appears directed toward the mediating figure, - and narrative distinctions between divine and angelic speech become difficult to separate linguistically. The purpose of this section is not to impose later doctrinal conclusions upon the Torahic material, but to examine carefully how the texts themselves construct the relationship between God, revelation, mediation, and sacred presence within the narrative structure of the Torah. Later comparative sections of the research will also examine how traditions of mediation develop across later Abrahamic texts and theological systems.

Section VI

Divine Speech and Revelation in the Torah

The Torah presents God as a speaking deity whose relationship with humanity is established through revelation, commandment, covenant, warning, promise, judgment, and sacred instruction. Divine speech functions as one of the central foundations through which the Torah constructs religious authority, prophetic legitimacy, and theological identity. Within the Torahic narrative, revelation appears through multiple forms, including: - direct divine speech, - mediated revelation, - prophetic communication, - sacred law, - visions, - covenant declarations, - and symbolic manifestations connected to divine presence. God speaks to major prophetic figures such as: - Noah, - Abraham, - Moses, - Aaron, - and other covenantal representatives. The Torah repeatedly portrays revelation not merely as abstract theological knowledge, but as active intervention within history. Divine speech establishes laws, forms covenants, guides nations, issues judgments, and directs the religious structure of the community. Particular importance is given to Moses within the Torahic revelation structure. Moses appears as the central prophetic mediator through whom commandments, legislation, ritual structures, and covenantal authority are transmitted to the Israelites. The Torah also connects revelation closely with sacred space and divine manifestation, especially in contexts such as: - Mount Sinai, - the Tabernacle, - the Tent of Meeting, - the cloud and fire, - and the Ark of the Covenant. At the same time, the Torah contains varying portrayals concerning the manner of divine communication. Certain passages describe direct speech between God and human beings, while others present revelation through dreams, visions, angels, symbolic manifestations, or mediated speech. These variations generate important theological and textual questions concerning: - the nature of revelation, - the limits of human perception, - direct versus mediated communication, - prophetic authority, - sacred language, - and the relationship between transcendence and historical interaction. The Torah further presents revelation as deeply connected to law and covenant. Divine speech does not function merely as information, but as binding authority that organizes: - worship, - morality, - ritual purity, - social order, - justice, - national identity, - and covenantal obligation. Another central dimension of Torahic revelation concerns the preservation and transmission of sacred speech. Revelation becomes connected to memory, writing, ritual recitation, priestly authority, and communal continuity across generations. This section will later proceed toward deeper textual analysis concerning: - revelation structures, - prophetic mediation, - divine speech formulas, - sacred authority, - covenantal transmission, - and the development of revelation theology within the Torahic narrative framework. Comparative sections will also examine how concepts of revelation and sacred speech develop later within the Gospel traditions and the Qur’anic revelation structure.

Section VII

The Angel of the Lord and Divine Mediation in the Torah

One of the most significant theological and textual phenomena within the Torah concerns the figure commonly described as “the Angel of the Lord.” In several passages, the narrative presents a complex and fluid relationship between the speech of the angel and the speech of God Himself. At certain moments, the text introduces: - the angel, - the messenger of God, - or a mediating figure, yet the narrative subsequently shifts into direct divine speech without always maintaining a clear distinction between the messenger and God. This phenomenon appears in multiple narrative contexts involving: - revelation, - covenant, - warning, - guidance, - salvation, - and sacred encounter. The figure of the Angel of the Lord appears especially within stories connected to: - Hagar, - Abraham, - Moses, - Balaam, - and the wilderness traditions. In some passages, the angel speaks in the first person as though speaking fully with divine authority. In other passages, the text moves between: - “the Angel of the Lord,” - “God,” - and “the Lord” within the same narrative structure. These textual movements raise major interpretive questions concerning: - divine mediation, - sacred representation, - prophetic transmission, - the authority of the messenger, - and the boundaries between divine presence and mediated revelation. The Torahic presentation may suggest that certain forms of revelation are delivered through a representative figure who speaks completely in the name of God and carries divine authority within the narrative structure. At the same time, the texts do not always provide systematic theological clarification concerning the precise nature of this mediation. As a result, later Jewish, Christian, and Islamic interpretive traditions approached these passages in different ways according to their theological frameworks. The study of the Angel of the Lord therefore becomes important not only for understanding Torahic revelation itself, but also for examining the historical development of concepts related to: - revelation, - mediation, - prophecy, - divine speech, - sacred authority, - and the relationship between God and heavenly messengers. This section will later examine the linguistic, theological, and narrative dimensions of these passages through detailed textual analysis, especially concerning: - direct and mediated revelation, - shifts in narrative voice, - sacred authority, - angelic representation, - covenantal communication, - and the relationship between divine transcendence and historical manifestation. Comparative analysis will also explore how later Abrahamic traditions reinterpret or restructure the concept of divine mediation and sacred messengers within their own theological systems.

Section VIII

Divine Names and Attributes in the Torah

The divine names and attributes within the Torah form one of the central foundations through which the text constructs its understanding of God, revelation, authority, covenant, and sacred presence. The Torah does not present divine names merely as titles or identifiers, but as theological expressions connected to divine action, power, holiness, mercy, judgment, revelation, and historical intervention. Among the major divine names appearing within the Torah are: - YHWH, - Elohim, - El, - El Shaddai, - Adonai, - and Lord of Hosts. Each name appears within specific theological, narrative, covenantal, or revelatory contexts, contributing to the broader structure through which the Torah presents divine identity. The name YHWH occupies a particularly central position within the Torahic tradition and becomes deeply associated with: - covenant, - revelation, - liberation, - sacred law, - historical intervention, - and divine presence among the Israelites. Other divine titles such as Elohim and El often appear within broader contexts connected to: - creation, - authority, - power, - judgment, - and universal sovereignty. The Torah also presents God through a wide range of attributes, including: - holiness, - mercy, - justice, - jealousy, - compassion, - power, - wisdom, - patience, - wrath, - forgiveness, - and covenantal faithfulness. These attributes function not only as descriptions of divine character, but also as organizing principles shaping: - sacred law, - worship, - covenant structure, - prophetic authority, - communal identity, - and the relationship between God and humanity. At the same time, the Torah contains passages where divine attributes appear in tension with one another. Certain narratives emphasize mercy and forgiveness, while others emphasize judgment, destruction, jealousy, punishment, or divine wrath. Additional theological tensions emerge concerning: - transcendence and anthropomorphic description, - invisibility and manifestation, - immutability and divine regret, - mercy and collective punishment, - and universal authority versus covenantal particularity. The study of divine names and attributes therefore extends beyond lexical analysis alone. It also involves examining how the Torah constructs the image of God through: - narrative structure, - sacred language, - covenant theology, - revelation, - historical action, - and theological symbolism. Particular attention will later be given to: - the theological function of divine names, - the relationship between names and revelation, - the evolution of divine titles, - sacred naming traditions, - and the comparative development of divine attributes across the Abrahamic traditions. This section will also examine how later interpretive traditions understood, translated, preserved, or transformed these divine names and attributes within broader theological systems.

Section IX

The Relationship Between God and Humanity in the Torah

The Torah presents the relationship between God and humanity as one of the central foundations of the religious and narrative structure of the text. Human beings are not portrayed merely as passive creations, but as moral, covenantal, and historical participants whose existence is directly connected to divine command, responsibility, judgment, and guidance. The Torah introduces humanity through the creation narratives, where human beings appear as creatures formed by divine will and placed within a structured relationship involving: - life, - obedience, - stewardship, - sacred law, - moral accountability, - and covenantal obligation.
